Showdown - The Rules Explained

Confused? Don't know where to begin? Here's a quick text primer to get you started.

For the full list of rules, please visit the Rules and Downloads page on the IBSA website (Opens in new tab) for more info.

This is an overview and should not be used as a substitution for the full rules. Consult these for tournament-specific rules and procedures.

Aim of the Game

  • Showdown is played on a rectangular table with raised edges, a centre screen and a goal at each end.
  • Players hit the ball across the table's surface, under the centre screen, using a wooden bat.
  • Your aim is to hit the ball into your opponent's goal.
  • You must defend your goal area against your opponent's hits.
  • Matches can be best of 1, 3 or 5 sets.
  • After a set, players change ends by walking round the right side of the table (anticlockwise). Change of ends lasts for 60 seconds
  • In the 3rd set of a 3-set match or 5th set of a 5-set match, players change ends when one player has scored 6 points.
  • First to 11 points, with a 2-point lead, wins the set. If the score is 11:10, you carry on playing until there is a 2-point margin between opponents.

Equipment

The Table

  • The table measures 3.66 metres by 1.22 metres.
  • Tables usually come in 2 halves, joined by clips or bolts.
  • There is a centre screen in the middle, with a gap underneath.
  • Raised side walls run along the two long sides of the table.
  • The backboard is positioned on the shorter sides of the table (end walls), either side of the goal pocket.
  • The contact board runs along the top of each end wall, including the backboard and goal pocket.
  • The goal area consists of the goal pocket, either made of plastic, netting or a mixture of both, and a semicircular tactile line in front of the goal.
  • Rounded curves connect the side walls and end walls.
  • The playing area consists of the playing surface, including the goal pockets; the side walls and area between side walls, including the top; the inside curves and inside of the backboard, as well as the bottom of the contact board.

Bats, Masks and Gloves

  • Bats are usually made of wood, though the handle can be 3-D printed or made of another solid material. If the blade is made of a non-wooden material, it needs to be covered by a soft material.
  • Bats can be a maximum of 30 cm, with a maximum thickness of 10 mm on the blade (soft coverings can be 2 mm on either side but can't exceed the 10 cm maximum).
  • Masks need to totally obscure your vision. Sleep masks , soft masks or masks that let in any light are not allowed in competition.
  • If you cannot wear rigid or semi-rigid masks, you need to wear patches on your eyes to obscure your vision totally during competitions. You can't remove the patches until the whole match is over.
  • Gloves, like field hockey gloves, must protect the whole hand: they need to cover the fingers and over the fingertips, back of the hand and palm.
  • Gloves can't increase the size of your hand by more than 2 cm on each side.
  • For examples and more details, visit the resources page.

What to Wear

  • Wear sports clothes that you can move in easily.
  • You can only wear long sleeves under your short-sleeved top if they are a different colour to your short-sleeved shirt, hand and arm protection.
  • Wearing sports shoes is advised. You shouldn't wear high heels or sandals.
  • You can wear wristbands or other forms of arm protection. See the resources page for details.
  • You shouldn't wear jewellery such as necklaces, as this could restrict your movement or lead to invasions (a foul).

    • Play

    Serves

    • On a serve, you can position the ball with your non-batting hand and hit with your batting hand.
    • You must serve within 2 seconds of the whistle blowing.
    • Once you have sent your serve, take your non-batting hand out of the playing area.
    • To serve, the ball must make contact with either the left or right side wall exactly once before it passes under the centre screen.
    • You must serve the ball after the whistle has blown, not before.
    • Each player serves twice before the service moves on to the other player
    • If there is a dead ball, where the ball is silent or unable to be reached for 2 seconds, the ball is re-served.
    • Players can score or foul regardless of who served the ball.
    • In 3 or 5-set matches, the starting server alternates.

    Rallies

    • After a player serves, you do not have to stick to the rule of hitting the ball once before the centre screen.
    • You can hit the ball against the side walls (bounce) once, twice or even three times.
    • When you stop the ball after defending, you can only hold it still (making no noise) for a maximum of 2 seconds.
    • Controlling the ball, where it makes audible noise, does not count towards the 2-second limit.
    • If your bat breaks, play will stop and any points scored after the break won't count.

    Goals

    • Goals are worth 2 points.
    • A goal is counted when the ball passes into the goal pocket. If it bounces off the posts, skims over the goal or doesn't fully go into the goal pocket, it won't count.
    • If the ball hits any part of your body, above your hand protection and then goes into the goal, it is counted as a body touch (a foul).
    • Either player can score a goal, regardless of who is serving.

    Fouls

    • Fouls are worth 1 point.
    • Either player can cause a foul against themselves or their opponent. Common ones are listed below.
    • Irregular serve (short): bouncing the ball twice or more against the side walls before it passes under the centre screen.
    • Irregular serve (long): not bouncing the ball at all against the side walls before it passes under the centre screen.
    • Body touch: where the ball hits any part of your body that is over the playing surface, above your hand protection.
    • Screen/centreboard: where the ball hits the screen and comes back to the player. If the ball passes under the screen, play carries on.
    • Out: where the ball leaves the table. It can hit the floor, ceiling, walls, a person or the top of the contact board. The point goes to the opponent of whoever touched the ball last, either with their bat or glove.
    • Invasion: where any part of your body that's outside the playing surface touches the inside of the table. For example, your non-playing hand going over the front edge of the contact board; your arm hitting the playing surface; your head or chest making contact with the playing surface.
    • Illegal defense: if your bat or glove make contact with the ball on, or behind, the tactile line or anywhere else in the goal area. If it passes into the goal directly after this happened, a goal will be called instead.
    • Bat infraction: if you drop your bat or hold your bat with two hands within the playing area.
    • Ball infraction: if you deliberately hold the ball so that it is silent for more than 2 seconds.

    Warnings and Penalties

    • There are some actions that can result in a warning, then a 2-point penalty if repeated. There are some which result in an immediate 2-point penalty.
    • Warnings are valid for the whole match. If you get a warning for one infraction, anything else that you do after which is warning-worthy will result in a 2-point penalty.
    • Warning: playing from the side of the table.
    • Warning: hooking a ball with your finger or controlling the ball with your fingers/thumb.
    • Warning: excessively pushing the table, so much so that it moves.
    • Warning: scraping your bat excessively or in an obstructive way.
    • Warning: delaying the game deliberately.
    • Warning: Talking during points and before the whistle has blown, including profanity.
    • 2-point penalty: touching your mask without asking the referee. You can only touch your mask without asking during timeouts and change of ends. You must turn 180 degrees away from the table when doing this.
    • 2-point penalty: your phone or your coach's phone ringing during a match.
    • 2-point penalty: the coach giving secret signs or coded messages to the player.
    • 2-point penalty: excessive abuse towards the other player or referee, including swearing, throwing bats or balls out of anger etc. Continuing to do this could result in disqualification for the match.

    timeouts and Coaches

    • Each player is allowed 1 timeout per set, which last 60 seconds.
    • Each player is allowed 1 coach, who stands at the side of the table, opposite the referee. The referee needs to be able to see the coach at all times.
    • Coaches can only talk to the player before the coin toss, during timeouts or change of ends.
    • If the coach calls a timeout for their player, that player has used their 1 allotted timeout per set and can't call another one until the next set.
    • Coaches and spectators can leave the room during timeouts and change of ends.

    Team Games

    • Team games differ from individual games in a few ways:
    • There are 3 players in each team, with at least 1 man and 1 woman.
    • Players have 3 serves each and rotate after they have served 3 times.
    • The team wins when they reach a score of 31, with a 2-point lead.
    • During the whole match, players can't touch their masks without asking the referee, except in timeouts or the change of ends.
    • Teams change ends at 16 points.

    Refereeing

    • Referees in competitions and tournaments must be fully sighted.
    • Before each match, referees check the players' equipment including bats, masks and gloves.
    • The referee will announce the player and any coaches and then toss a coin to choose who will serve first. Players have the option of choosing to serve, or to change ends.
    • Before each point, the referee checks if the players are ready (only in specific circumstances), announces the score and who is serving, before blowing the whistle once. For example: "First serve Anna. The score is 0:0".
    • Referees will only ask the players if they are ready at the start of the match, after timeouts are ended, after change of ends or after an unusually long break.
    • The score is led by whoever is serving. For example: "Second serve Chris. The score is 0:2".
    • When a point is scored, the referee blows the whistle once for a foul or twice for a goal.
    • The referee will then announce what happened: "Goal Anna, 2 points to Anna", or "Body touch Chris, 1 point to Anna".
    • If the referee is unsure of a call, they will say 'let' and re-serve the ball.
    • Referees will announce 15 seconds before the timeout or change of ends finishes, and when it has finished.
    • When the set ends, the referee will blow the whistle once or twice depending on if the game ended on a foul or goal, then they will blow a long final whistle to signal the set's end.
    • They will announce the final score of the set and who won, before asking players to change ends. If it is the end of the match, the referee will announce who won the match. For example: "Anna wins the match, 2 sets to 1".
